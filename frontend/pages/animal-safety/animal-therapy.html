What is Animal-Assisted Therapy?

Animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is a goal-oriented intervention that intentionally incorporates animals as an integral part of the therapeutic process. This evidence-based practice has shown remarkable results in improving physical, emotional, social, and cognitive functioning in people of all ages.

Common Therapy Animals

Dogs

The most common therapy animals, dogs provide emotional support, reduce stress, and assist in physical rehabilitation. Breeds like Golden Retrievers and Labrador Retrievers are particularly effective.

  • PTSD support
  • Autism assistance
  • Depression relief

Cats

Cats offer calming companionship and are excellent for individuals who prefer quieter, less energetic interactions. Their purring has therapeutic effects.

  • Anxiety reduction
  • Blood pressure regulation
  • Loneliness prevention

Horses

Equine-assisted therapy is powerful for physical rehabilitation, building confidence, and developing emotional regulation skills.

  • Physical therapy
  • Emotional healing
  • Confidence building

Birds

Birds, especially parrots, can help improve communication skills and provide engaging companionship for the elderly and those with cognitive challenges.

  • Speech therapy
  • Cognitive stimulation
  • Social engagement

Fish & Aquariums

Observing fish in aquariums has proven calming effects, reducing blood pressure and anxiety in therapeutic settings.

  • Stress reduction
  • Meditation aid
  • Dementia care

Dolphins

Dolphin-assisted therapy is used for children with developmental disabilities and provides unique sensory experiences.

  • Autism support
  • Motor skills development
  • Emotional connection

Types of Animal-Assisted Therapy Programs

Therapy animals visit hospitals to comfort patients, reduce pre-surgery anxiety, and support recovery. Programs include:

  • Pediatric hospital visits
  • Cancer treatment support
  • Rehabilitation centers
  • Emergency room therapy
  • Palliative care programs
Success Story: Studies show that animal visits before surgery can reduce anxiety by up to 40% and decrease the need for pain medication post-surgery.

Animals in schools help create supportive learning environments and assist students with various challenges:

  • Reading programs (children read to therapy dogs)
  • Special education support
  • Stress relief during exams
  • Social skills development
  • Anti-bullying initiatives
Impact: Reading to dogs has been shown to improve literacy skills by up to 12% and boost children's confidence significantly.

Animal-assisted therapy is integrated into mental health treatment plans for:

  • PTSD and trauma recovery
  • Addiction treatment programs
  • Eating disorder treatment
  • Depression and anxiety management
  • Suicide prevention programs
Research Finding: Veterans with PTSD who participated in animal-assisted therapy showed 82% reduction in symptoms after 12 weeks.

Therapy animals bring joy and purpose to elderly residents:

  • Dementia and Alzheimer's care
  • Depression prevention
  • Physical activity encouragement
  • Memory stimulation
  • Social engagement programs
Benefit: Regular animal visits in nursing homes have been linked to reduced use of psychotropic medications by up to 50%.

Specialized programs designed for individuals with autism and developmental challenges:

  • Service dogs for autism
  • Sensory integration therapy
  • Communication skill development
  • Behavioral management support
  • Social interaction training
Evidence: Children with autism who interact with therapy animals show improved social skills and reduced problem behaviors.

Animals assist in physical therapy and rehabilitation:

  • Equine-assisted physical therapy
  • Stroke rehabilitation
  • Spinal cord injury recovery
  • Traumatic brain injury treatment
  • Motor skill development
Outcome: Hippotherapy (horse therapy) improves balance, coordination, and muscle strength more effectively than traditional therapy alone.

The Science Behind Animal Therapy

Biochemical Changes

Interacting with animals triggers the release of oxytocin (the "love hormone"), reduces cortisol (stress hormone), and increases serotonin and dopamine levels, improving overall mood and well-being.

Physiological Responses

Petting animals has been shown to lower blood pressure, decrease heart rate, and regulate breathing patterns, creating a calming physiological state.

Neurological Impact

Brain imaging studies show that interacting with animals activates the prefrontal cortex and limbic system, areas associated with emotion regulation and social bonding.

Psychological Benefits

Animals provide non-judgmental support, creating a safe environment for emotional expression and helping individuals develop trust and empathy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Therapy Animals: Trained to provide comfort to multiple people in settings like hospitals and schools. They do not have public access rights.

Service Animals: Individually trained to perform specific tasks for one person with a disability. They have legal public access rights.

Emotional Support Animals: Provide comfort through companionship but are not trained for specific tasks. They have limited legal protections.

Not every animal is suitable for therapy work. Ideal therapy animals must have:

  • Calm and friendly temperament
  • Comfort around strangers and in new environments
  • Ability to be handled by multiple people
  • Good health and hygiene
  • Proper training and certification

Most organizations have specific requirements and testing protocols to ensure animals are suitable for therapy work.

Coverage varies by insurance provider and plan. Some insurance companies cover animal-assisted therapy when it's:

  • Prescribed by a licensed healthcare provider
  • Part of a documented treatment plan
  • Conducted by certified professionals
  • Integrated with traditional therapy methods

It's best to contact your insurance provider directly to inquire about specific coverage for animal-assisted therapy programs.

Results vary depending on the individual and the condition being treated. However:

  • Immediate effects: Reduced stress and improved mood can be observed during the first session
  • Short-term (weeks): Improvements in social interaction and communication skills
  • Long-term (months): Significant progress in therapeutic goals, physical rehabilitation, and mental health outcomes

Consistency and regular sessions typically yield the best results.

While generally safe, animal-assisted therapy may not be appropriate for individuals with:

  • Severe animal allergies
  • Animal phobias
  • Compromised immune systems (consult with doctor first)
  • History of animal aggression or abuse

All reputable programs conduct thorough assessments before beginning therapy and maintain strict health and safety protocols for both animals and participants.

To find animal therapy programs near you:

  • Contact major therapy animal organizations like Pet Partners or TDI
  • Ask your healthcare provider for referrals
  • Check with local hospitals, nursing homes, and schools
  • Search online directories of certified therapy animal teams
  • Contact your local humane society or animal shelter
  • Reach out to universities with animal science or therapy programs
