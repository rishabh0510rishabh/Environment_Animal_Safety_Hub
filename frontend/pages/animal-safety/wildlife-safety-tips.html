Wildlife Safety Tips

Respecting wildlife means keeping themwild and keeping yourself safe. Learn how to coexist responsibly in their natural habitat.

Why Wildlife Safety Matters

Most conflicts occur when animals feel threatened, cornered, or attracted by food. By understanding animal behavior and following safety protocols, we can enjoy nature without disturbing its inhabitants.

Keep Your Distance

Use the "Thumb Rule": If you can cover the entire animal with your thumb at arm's length, you are at a safe distance. If not, back away slowly.

Never Feed Wildlife

Feeding animals makes them lose their natural fear of humans, leading to aggressive behavior. "A fed bear is a dead bear."

Stay Alert

Avoid wearing headphones. Make noise while hiking (talk, clap, sing) to avoid surprising animals, especially near rushing water or dense brush.

DO

  • Carry bear spray and know how to use it.
  • Store food in bear-resistant containers.
  • Keep pets on a leash at all times.
  • Hike in groups (safety in numbers).
  • Back away slowly if you encounter a predator.

DON'T

  • Don't run! Running triggers a chase instinct.
  • Don't approach baby animals; the mother is nearby.
  • Don't leave trash or food scraps behind.
  • Don't hike alone at dawn or dusk (peak activity times).
  • Don't corner an animal; always leave an escape route.

Pro Tips per Animal

Bears

Black Bear: Make noise, make yourself big, fight back if attacked.
Grizzly: Do NOT fight back. Play dead (protect neck) if it makes contact.

Cougars

Maintain eye contact. Do not crouch or turn your back. Make yourself look huge. Throw objects if it approaches.

Moose/Elk

They defend personal space aggressively. If charged, run and get behind a tree or large rock.

Snakes

Watch where you step. If you see one, freeze, then slowly back away. Give it a wide berth. Most bites happen when people try to kill or move them.
