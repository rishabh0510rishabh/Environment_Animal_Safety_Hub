Nature's Most Valuable Ecosystems

Wetlands are among the most productive and biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth. These vital habitats serve as natural water filters, carbon sinks, and breeding grounds for countless species.

Water Purification

Wetlands act as natural filters, removing pollutants, excess nutrients, and sediments from water before it reaches rivers and oceans.

Carbon Storage

These ecosystems store massive amounts of carbon, helping mitigate climate change while providing habitat for diverse plant and animal life.

Flood Control

Wetlands absorb excess water during storms and floods, reducing damage to communities and infrastructure downstream.

Diverse Wetland Ecosystems

Forested Wetlands

Swamps and bottomland hardwood forests that provide critical habitat for migratory birds and filter water from surrounding landscapes.

Marshlands

Grassy wetlands that serve as breeding grounds for amphibians, fish, and waterfowl, while stabilizing shorelines and improving water quality.

Bogs and Fens

Peat-accumulating wetlands that store carbon and provide unique habitats for specialized plant and animal communities.

Estuaries

Where freshwater meets saltwater, creating highly productive ecosystems that support commercial and recreational fisheries.

Major Threats to Wetlands

Urban Development

Rapid urbanization and infrastructure expansion destroys wetlands through direct conversion and increased stormwater runoff.

Agriculture

Drainage for farming and excessive fertilizer use leads to nutrient pollution and habitat loss across agricultural landscapes.

Pollution

Industrial waste, pesticides, and plastic pollution contaminate wetland ecosystems and harm wildlife populations.

Climate Change

Rising sea levels, altered precipitation patterns, and increased storm intensity threaten coastal and inland wetlands alike.

Overexploitation

Unsustainable harvesting of fish, shellfish, and other wetland resources depletes populations and disrupts ecosystem balance.

Invasive Species

Non-native plants and animals outcompete native species, altering wetland structure and reducing biodiversity.

Wetlands as Biodiversity Hotspots

Bird Migration

Over 40% of North American bird species rely on wetlands for breeding, feeding, or resting during migration. Coastal wetlands alone support millions of migratory birds annually.

Fish Nurseries

Many commercially important fish species spend their juvenile stages in wetlands, where they find protection from predators and abundant food sources.

Amphibian Habitats

Wetlands provide critical breeding habitats for amphibians, which are among the most threatened groups of animals worldwide.

Wetland Restoration Success Stories

Everglades Restoration

The world's largest wetland restoration project aims to restore natural water flow to the Florida Everglades, benefiting wildlife and improving water quality.

Bottomland Forests

Restoration of floodplain forests along major rivers has improved water quality, reduced flooding, and enhanced wildlife habitat.

Coastal Marshes

Marsh creation and restoration projects protect shorelines from erosion and provide critical habitat for fish and shellfish populations.

Urban Wetlands

Constructed wetlands in urban areas treat stormwater, improve air quality, and provide green spaces for community enjoyment.

The Economic Value of Wetlands

Fisheries and Recreation

Wetlands support commercial and recreational fisheries worth billions annually, while providing opportunities for hunting, birdwatching, and ecotourism.

Flood Protection

Healthy wetlands reduce flood damage costs by absorbing stormwater and reducing the need for expensive flood control infrastructure.

Water Quality

Wetlands improve drinking water quality and reduce treatment costs for municipalities and industries downstream.

Protect Our Wetlands

Wetlands are irreplaceable ecosystems that provide essential services to humanity. Their conservation is crucial for our environmental and economic future.
