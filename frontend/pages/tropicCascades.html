The domino effect in ecological systems where predators regulate ecosystem structure and function

Lion Decline in African Savannas

Serengeti, Africa
Lion Population Decline
Baboon Population Increase
Crop Damage & Human Conflict
Disease Transmission Increase
43% Lion decline in 20 years
3x Baboon population increase
$1M+ Annual crop damage

As lion populations decline due to habitat loss and human conflict, mesopredator populations like baboons explode. These baboons raid crops, increasing human-wildlife conflict, and serve as disease reservoirs.

Consequences:

  • Increased crop damage and economic losses
  • Higher parasite and disease transmission to humans
  • Reduced livestock safety
  • Changed herbivore grazing patterns

  • Migratory bird nest predation patterns
  • Soil nutrient cycling variations
  • Climate change disrupting natural cycles

Boreal Forest: Lynx and Hare

Canadian Boreal Forest
Classic Lotka-Volterra Dynamics
dH/dt = rH - aHP
dP/dt = baHP - mP
H Hare population
P Lynx population
r Hare growth rate
a Predation rate
10 years Cycle period
30x Hare population swing
9-12 months Predator response lag

The textbook example of predator-prey dynamics documented for over 100 years. Snowshoe hare populations cycle every 10 years, driving Canada lynx population cycles with a time lag.

Consequences:

  • Forest understory vegetation cycles with hare populations
  • Other predators (coyotes, owls) follow lynx patterns
  • Fur trade economics tied to natural cycles
  • Climate change affecting cycle timing and amplitude

Island Ecosystems: Rats and Seabirds

Pacific Islands
Rat Introduction
Seabird Decline
Nutrient Loss
Forest Decline
90% Seabird decline with rats
75% Reduced nutrient input
50+ Island restoration projects

Introduced rats prey on seabird eggs and chicks, causing seabird population crashes. This reduces nutrient inputs (guano) that fertilize island ecosystems, leading to forest decline and reduced biodiversity.

Consequences:

  • Coastal forest die-off without seabird nutrients
  • Reduced soil fertility and changed plant communities
  • Coral reef impacts from reduced nutrient runoff
  • Successful restoration through rat eradication

Human Role in Trophic Cascades

How human activities trigger and can help reverse trophic cascades

Human-Induced Cascades

High Impact

Primary Causes:

  • Overhunting of apex predators
  • Habitat fragmentation
  • Invasive species introduction
  • Pollution and climate change

Example: Shark Finning

Global shark populations declined by 70%, leading to mesopredator release and disrupted marine food webs.

100M sharks/year killed 70% population decline

Restoration Successes

Successful

Restoration Methods:

  • Predator reintroduction programs
  • Protected area establishment
  • Invasive species eradication
  • Habitat corridor creation

Example: European Lynx

Reintroduction to Alps and Carpathians reduced overabundant deer and allowed forest regeneration.

2,000 lynx reintroduced 50% deer reduction
