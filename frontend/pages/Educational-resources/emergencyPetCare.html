Emergency Pet Care

Be prepared for pet emergencies. Learn how to recognize critical situations, administer basic first aid, and know when to seek immediate veterinary care. Your quick response could save your pet's life.

Recognize Emergency Signs

These symptoms require IMMEDIATE veterinary attention. Don't wait - every minute counts.

Breathing Difficulties

Life-threatening respiratory issues

  • Gasping for air or choking
  • Blue or pale gums/tongue
  • Wheezing or labored breathing
  • Rapid shallow breathing
  • Open-mouth breathing (in cats)

Neurological Issues

Signs of neurological emergencies

  • Seizures or convulsions
  • Sudden collapse or paralysis
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Severe disorientation
  • Head tilt or circling

Bleeding & Trauma

Serious injuries and blood loss

  • Uncontrolled bleeding
  • Deep wounds or lacerations
  • Broken bones or limping
  • Blood in urine or stool
  • Vomiting blood

Severe Pain & Distress

Signs of extreme pain or suffering

  • Crying, whining, or howling
  • Extreme agitation or restlessness
  • Hiding or seeking isolation
  • Aggression when touched
  • Abdominal tenderness

Emergency First Aid Steps

What to do while getting to the veterinarian. Remember: Your safety first!

1

Assess Safety First

Approach injured pets cautiously. Even friendly pets may bite when in pain. Use a muzzle if necessary (never for breathing difficulties, vomiting, or seizures). Cover with blanket for handling.

2

Check ABCs

Airway: Clear mouth of obstructions. Breathing: Watch for chest movement. Circulation: Check gums - pink = good, white/blue = emergency. Check pulse on inner thigh.

3

Control Bleeding

Apply direct pressure with clean cloth. Elevate wound if possible. Use pressure points for severe bleeding. For arterial bleeding (spurting blood), apply tourniquet above wound as last resort.

4

Stabilize for Transport

Use rigid surface as stretcher (board, baking sheet). Secure pet with blankets/towels. Keep neck/spine aligned if trauma suspected. Keep pet warm during transport.

5

Call Ahead & Transport

Call emergency clinic while en route. Bring any vomit, stool, or suspected poison. Bring medications and medical records. Have someone else drive if possible.

Common household toxins and what to do if ingestion is suspected

Human Medications

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
  • Antidepressants
  • ADHD medications
  • Blood pressure medications

Household Chemicals

  • Antifreeze (extremely toxic)
  • Rodenticides
  • Cleaning products
  • Fertilizers
  • Insecticides

Food Toxins

  • Chocolate (especially dark)
  • Xylitol (sugar-free gum)
  • Grapes & raisins
  • Onions & garlic
  • Alcohol

Plants & Other

  • Lilies (extremely toxic to cats)
  • Sago palms
  • Oleander
  • Azaleas/rhododendrons
  • Macadamia nuts

Emergency Kit Essentials

Prepare your pet first aid kit before an emergency happens

Wound Care

Gauze pads, adhesive tape, bandages, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, hydrogen peroxide (3%), self-adhesive wrap

Tools & Instruments

Digital thermometer, tweezers, scissors, syringe (for giving liquids), muzzle, blanket, flashlight, disposable gloves

Medical Information

Vet's phone number, emergency clinic info, pet's medical records, current medications list, vaccination records

Medications

Activated charcoal (vet-approved), Benadryl (dosage from vet), styptic powder, saline solution, vet-prescribed emergency meds
