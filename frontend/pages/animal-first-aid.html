Animal First Aid & Emergency Care

Essential first aid knowledge to help injured animals before professional care arrives

‚ö° Quick Emergency Assessment (ABC Method)

A
Airway
Check if breathing passage is clear. Remove visible obstructions carefully.
B
Breathing
Look for chest movement. Normal: 15-30 breaths/min for dogs, 20-40 for cats.
C
Circulation
Check pulse on inner thigh. Look for bleeding, pale gums, or shock signs.
D
Disability
Check for spinal injuries, broken bones, or inability to move limbs.

Emergency First Aid Procedures

ü©∏
Bleeding Control
  1. Apply direct pressure with clean cloth/gauze
  2. Elevate injured area above heart if possible
  3. Do NOT remove embedded objects
  4. If bleeding soaks through, add more layers
  5. Apply pressure bandage to maintain pressure
  6. Seek immediate veterinary care
üí®
Choking/Airway Obstruction
  1. Open mouth and look for visible objects
  2. Use tweezers/pliers to remove if visible
  3. Never use fingers - may push deeper
  4. For small animals: hold upside down, tap back
  5. For large animals: lift hind legs, compress chest
  6. Perform rescue breathing if needed
ü¶¥
Fractures & Broken Bones
  1. Do NOT try to set or move broken bones
  2. Keep animal calm and still
  3. Support injured limb with towels/blankets
  4. Create makeshift splint only if necessary for transport
  5. Use board or firm surface to move animal
  6. Transport to vet immediately
üî•
Burns & Heat Injuries
  1. Remove from heat source immediately
  2. Cool burn with cool (not cold) water for 10-15 min
  3. Never use ice, butter, or oils
  4. Cover with clean, damp cloth
  5. Do not break blisters or remove stuck material
  6. Monitor for shock symptoms
üßä
Hypothermia & Cold Exposure
  1. Move to warm, dry environment
  2. Wrap in warm blankets or towels
  3. Apply warm (not hot) water bottles to body
  4. Warm gradually - avoid direct heat
  5. Check for frostbite on ears, paws, tail
  6. Monitor breathing and heart rate
‚ò†Ô∏è
Poisoning
  1. Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed
  2. Remove animal from poison source
  3. Collect poison container/sample if safe
  4. Call Poison Control: 1-800-213-6680
  5. Rinse mouth/skin with water if external
  6. Rush to emergency vet immediately

Safe Animal Handling

üêï
Injured Dogs
Approach slowly from the side, speak softly. Use a blanket or towel to wrap and lift. Support head and body. Create a muzzle with gauze if animal is conscious and may bite.
‚ö†Ô∏è Even friendly dogs may bite when injured and scared
üê±
Injured Cats
Use thick towel or blanket to wrap cat securely. Support body and head. Place in carrier or box with air holes. Cats hide pain well - handle very gently.
‚ö†Ô∏è Cats can inflict serious scratches and bites when frightened
ü¶Ö
Birds
Use towel to gently cover bird. Support body, avoid pressure on chest. Keep in dark, quiet box with air holes. Handle minimally - birds stress easily.
‚ö†Ô∏è Raptors have powerful talons - use extreme caution
üê∞
Small Animals (Rabbits, etc.)
Support hindquarters and chest. Never pick up by ears or scruff. Keep calm and quiet - small animals can die from stress alone.
‚ö†Ô∏è Rabbits can break their backs if they kick while being held

Quick Reference

1. ASSESS: ABC + Disability
2. SECURE: Make safe for you & animal

4. TRANSPORT: Get to vet ASAP

When NOT to Help

  • Animal is aggressive and poses danger
  • You lack proper protective equipment
  • Scene is unsafe (traffic, fire, etc.)
  • Wild animal appears healthy (may be normal behavior)
  • Mother animal is nearby (may be protecting young)
  • You're unsure - call professionals first
Remember: Your safety comes first. A second victim doesn't help anyone.
